Circuit Breakers — Closed / Open / Half-Open

A client-side resiliency pattern that fail-fasts calls to a failing downstream once errors cross a threshold — so the caller's threads don't hang on 30s timeouts and the sick dependency gets room to recover. Watch a request travel the wire as the breaker trips and heals.

Real-world analogy — the MCB in your house

Think of the MCB (circuit breaker switch) in your home's wiring. Normally it sits closed — current flows, every appliance runs. If a short circuit or overload hits, the MCB instantly trips open and cuts the current, intentionally breaking the circuit so the wiring doesn't melt and the house doesn't catch fire. A software circuit breaker does exactly this between your service and a downstream API: healthy → closed, calls flow; downstream collapses → trip open, every new call fails instantly without even touching downstream; a while later it lets a tiny trial current through (half-open) — if downstream is healthy again, snap back closed; if not, open again. It protects both sides: the caller from hanging, and the downstream from a pile-on.

No breaker — cascading failure

500 QPS × 30s timeout = pile-up

Payment dies; every call hangs the full 30s timeout. At 500 QPS that's thousands of blocked threads. The threads never free, so checkout itself hangs and the failure cascades upstream — a "retry storm."

With breaker — fail-fast

trip in ~1-2s, then ~0.1ms reject

After ~1-2s the breaker trips Open. Now all 500 QPS fail or serve a fallback in ~0.1ms instead of waiting 30s. Threads stay free, checkout stays alive, and Payment gets load relief to recover.

Things to know in the interview

  • It doesn't fix downstream. A breaker only protects the caller (fail-fast, save threads) and gives the dependency load relief. Recovery is downstream's own job.
  • Breaker ≠ retry. A retry re-attempts the same request for transient errors; a breaker blocks future requests during sustained failure. "Try again" vs "don't try right now." They complement but do different jobs.
  • Half-open is the whole point of auto-recovery. Without it the breaker would stay Open forever (or need a manual reset). The single trial probe is what discovers downstream is healthy again.
  • Per-dependency isolation. Each downstream gets its own breaker — Payment tripping must not block Search calls. One global breaker is a classic mistake.
  • Minimum volume guards false trips. On low traffic, error rate alone makes 1/2 look like 100%. Require a minimum count (e.g. 20+ requests) before the rate can trip.
  • Timeout is mandatory. Without a per-call timeout a hanging call never counts as a "failure," so the breaker never trips. Timeout + breaker go together.
  • Real systems: Netflix Hystrix, Resilience4j (Java), Polly (.NET), and Envoy / Istio outlier detection. Often paired with retries and bulkheads.

Key takeaway

A circuit breaker is a 3-state machine — Closed (calls flow, failures counted), Open (fail-fast for a sleep window so the caller's threads aren't burned on 30s timeouts), and Half-Open (one trial probe that snaps back to Closed on success or back to Open on failure). It protects the caller and relieves the downstream — but never repairs downstream itself.
