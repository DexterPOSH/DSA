SLA / SLO / SLI — The Error-Budget Walkthrough

Three terms interviewers love to test. SLI is a measured number, SLO is an internal target on that number, and SLA is an external contract with penalties. Watch one service's 99.9% availability SLO spend its error budget across a rolling 28-day window — and decide ship-vs-freeze from it.

Real-world analogy — the "30 minutes or free" pizza shop

SLI the stopwatch reading on every order — "what % of today's deliveries arrived under 30 min." Raw measurement, truth as-is.

SLO the shop's internal target — "we want 95% of deliveries on time." A goal the team works toward; customers get nothing if it slips.

SLA the printed promise on the box — "late = pizza free." Breaking it costs the shop real money (a refund).

Key move: the internal target (SLO) is kept stricter than the public promise (SLA) so one late order never bankrupts the shop. SLI measures, SLO targets, SLA bills — that's the whole exam.

SLO stricter than SLA ✓

SLI ≈ 99.95%  ≥  SLO 99.9% > SLA 99.5%

The internal target sits above the public promise. The ~0.4% gap is a deliberate buffer: you can miss your own SLO, alarm internally, freeze features — and still never owe a refund. SLA missed = money out the door; SLO missed = an internal alarm only.

SLA tighter than SLO ✗

SLA 99.99% > SLO 99.9% (inverted!)

If you promise customers more than you target internally, you are designed to pay penalties on every normal SLO slip. Also fatal: setting SLO = 100% — error budget becomes zero, nobody can ever deploy, and cost goes infinite.

Things to know in the interview

  • Direction of strictness is the whole game: SLI (reality) ≈/≥ SLO (internal) > SLA (external). SLA must always be looser than SLO — that buffer is non-negotiable.
  • Error budget is meant to be spent. budget = 100% − SLO. A full budget means you're shipping too cautiously; spend it on risky-but-valuable launches. It's a currency, not a punishment quota.
  • Burn-rate alerting beats fixed thresholds — page when "budget is burning 5% in 2 hours," because it's SLO-aware instead of a blind error-count threshold.
  • Measure with percentiles, never averages. "Avg latency 50ms" hides the tail; p99 tells you what the worst 1% (often your revenue-critical users) actually feel.
  • Window choice matters. A 1-day window is noisy (one incident eats it all); a 28/30-day rolling window is smoother and doesn't snap-reset at a calendar boundary.
  • Denominator hygiene — exclude health-check traffic (it inflates the number); decide carefully whether 4xx client errors count against you.
  • Compose dependencies serially: two services in a chain → 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.998. Your SLO can't exceed what you depend on.

Key takeaway

SLI = the measured number, SLO = your internal target on it, SLA = the external promise with penalties. Keep them ordered reality ≥ SLO > SLA, turn the gap below 100% into an error budget (100% − SLO), and let that budget be the objective referee for "ship fast" vs "freeze and stabilise" — instead of opinions.
