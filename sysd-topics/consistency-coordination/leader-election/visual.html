Leader Election — Heartbeats, Terms & Quorum

How a cluster of nodes agrees on exactly one leader to order all writes, detects when that leader dies, and elects a fresh one — without ever ending up with two leaders (the dreaded split-brain).

Real-world analogy — the cricket captain

A team of 5 players needs one captain to call the toss and set the field — one decider means no confusion. While the captain keeps shouting instructions (a heartbeat every few seconds), everyone just plays. If the captain walks off injured and goes quiet for too long (the election timeout), the players hold a fresh vote and the next agreed player takes over — play never stops, only leadership hands off. The danger: if two players both yell "I'm captain!" at once, you get conflicting calls — that's split-brain, and the voting rule (a majority must agree) is exactly what stops it.

No quorum / no term

leader = "whoever declares first"

A network partition splits the cluster into 3 | 2. With no majority rule, each side elects its own leader and both accept writes. Result: 2 leaders — conflicting writes, corrupted data.

This is split-brain: the single most-asked leader-election failure in interviews.

Quorum + term + fencing

leader = wins majority in a new, higher term

On the same 3 | 2 split, only the 3-side can reach quorum (3 ≥ 3); the 2-side can never win (2 < 3). At most 1 leader per term — 3 + 3 > 5 makes two majorities impossible.

If the old leader returns, its lower term + stale fencing token get rejected and it demotes to follower.

Things to know in the interview

  • Why odd node counts? 3 → tolerates 1 failure, 5 → tolerates 2. A 4-node cluster also needs quorum 3 but still only tolerates 1 — extra cost, no extra safety. Always 3 / 5 / 7.
  • Heartbeat << election timeout. Heartbeat every 50–150 ms, timeout 150–300 ms. The gap absorbs normal network jitter so a blip doesn't trigger a needless election.
  • Randomized timeouts stop an election storm: if every follower times out together, votes split and nobody wins. Random per-node timeouts let one node ask first and win.
  • Missed heartbeat ≠ death. A GC pause or slow disk can delay a heartbeat. Fencing tokens (monotonic numbers) make a "dead" leader's late writes get rejected if it comes back.
  • Election restriction. Only a node with an up-to-date log can win — so a new leader never overwrites already-committed data.
  • Used by — Raft (etcd, CockroachDB, TiKV), ZooKeeper (epochs), Kafka (per-partition leader + controller / KRaft), and Kubernetes (controller-manager lease objects).

Key takeaway

Elect one leader to serialize writes; keep it alive with heartbeats; on silence past a randomized election timeout, bump the term and run a vote where a majority quorum is required to win. Because two majorities can't exist in one term, you get at most one leader at a time — and fencing tokens neutralize a zombie old leader.
